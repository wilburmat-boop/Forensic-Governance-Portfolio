MATTHEE v AFROCENTRIC / MEDSCHEME HOLDINGS

CONSOLIDATED DEFENCE: LRA · PDA · BCEA

Prepared by: EthicHawks Forensic Governance Consultancy
Date: June 2026
Status: Court‑Ready Forensic Submission
Evidence Base: 02_Evidence_Core (sealed), Annexure Map, Chronological Register

1. BACKGROUND

1.1 The Applicant (Wilbur William Matthee) was employed by the Respondent (AfroCentric Health / Medscheme Holdings) from 9 April 2024 until 12 December 2025, when he resigned amid constructive dismissal.

1.2 The Applicant was recruited for the role of Specialist: Care Manager, but was contractually demoted to Care Coordinator (Grade B4M1) in a bait‑and‑switch (Annexure A1).

1.3 The Applicant is a protected whistleblower who made several protected disclosures between October 2025 and January 2026 regarding the Gexus‑DMS system integration gap, financial irregularity, and patient safety risks.

1.4 The Respondent retaliated through salary deduction, harassment, gender‑identity discrimination, institutional silence, and procedural bad faith, leading to the Applicant’s constructive dismissal and subsequent litigation.

2. LABOUR RELATIONS ACT 66 OF 1995 (LRA)

2.1 Constructive Dismissal – Section 186(1)(e)

Legal test: The employee terminated the contract because the employer made continued employment intolerable.

Applicant’s case:

  • Unlawful salary deduction (40.47%) without consultation or statutory compliance (Annexures C‑1, C‑2). The deduction was implemented on 23 July 2025 and persisted despite repeated grievance (internal complaint 23 September 2025).
  • Gender identity discrimination: The Applicant was subjected to a degrading “Wilma” post, leading to a formal grievance that was never properly addressed (Annexures E1, E2).
  • Failure to accommodate disability: The Applicant provided medical evidence of severe depression and PTSD on 16 May 2025 (Dr Jacques Milan, Annexure B1). The Respondent ignored the accommodation request and later dismissed the clinical depression as a “cynical excuse” in legal proceedings (Annexure P1 – Perjury Schedule).
  • Retaliatory conduct after protected disclosures: the employer’s HR partner made a recorded admission of harassment and retaliation on 31 October 2025 (Annexure VICT1).

The cumulative effect of these actions rendered continued employment intolerable, forcing the Applicant to resign on 12 December 2025.

Supporting evidence: C‑1: Pay Slip Log (July 2025), C‑2: Technical Memo (09 Dec 2025), B1 / B1_malan: Psychological Impact Statement (Dr Jacques Milan), VICT1: Protected Disclosure recording (23 Oct 2025), E1/E2: Gender discrimination complaint.

2.2 Automatically Unfair Dismissal – Section 187(1)(h)

Reason: The dismissal (constructive) was based on the Applicant having made a protected disclosure under the PDA.

The timeline of events demonstrates a direct causal link between the protected disclosure of 23 October 2025 and the subsequent escalation of retaliation, culminating in resignation. The CCMA conciliation failed because the Commissioner refused to address the whistleblower dimension (Annexure C‑3).

2.3 Unfair Labour Practice – Section 186(2)

The Respondent committed an unfair labour practice relating to benefits (unlawful deduction) and disciplinary action short of dismissal (retaliatory performance warnings after the disclosure).

3. PROTECTED DISCLOSURES ACT 26 OF 2000 (PDA)

3.1 Protection of Whistleblower – Sections 2 & 3

The Applicant made a protected disclosure on 23 October 2025 to his employer (Group CEO Gerald van Wyk) regarding:

  • Gexus‑DMS system integration gap that allowed double billing and potential fraud against medical aid schemes.
  • Financial irregularity in the CMS‑GEMS payment architecture.
  • Patient safety risks arising from uncorrected system defects.

This disclosure satisfied the requirements of a protected disclosure: it was made in good faith, to the employer, and concerned a serious impropriety (criminal offence, health/safety risk, maladministration).

3.2 Occupational Detriment – Section 1

Following the disclosure, the Applicant suffered:

  • Unlawful salary reduction (continued)
  • Harassment and retaliation (admitted by employer’s HR partner)
  • Constructive dismissal
  • Refusal to provide a certificate of service
  • Fabricated disciplinary allegations post‑disclosure (Perjury Schedule P1‑P13)

These are all “occupational detriments” as defined in the PDA.

3.3 Remedies Sought – Section 4

The Applicant is entitled to:

  • Compensation for actual financial loss (salary difference, benefits)
  • Compensation for injury to dignity and reputation
  • An order directing the Respondent to cease the retaliatory conduct
  • Referral of criminal conduct (perjury, fraud) to the NPA

Supporting evidence: VICT1: Protected Disclosure recording / document, RA1: Reply to Opposing Affidavit (details the disclosure timeline), OA1: Opposing Affidavit response, P1‑P13: Perjury Schedule (false statements under oath).

4. BASIC CONDITIONS OF EMPLOYMENT ACT 75 OF 1997 (BCEA)

4.1 Unlawful Deduction – Section 34

The Respondent deducted 40.47% of the Applicant’s salary without written consent or legal authorisation, in violation of Section 34(1). The deduction was invoked under a false “no work no pay” claim despite the Applicant being ready and willing to work.

Evidence: Pay slips (C‑1), payslip June 2025 baseline (A2), internal grievance correspondence (C‑2).

4.2 Sick Leave and Disability Accommodation – Sections 22 & 23

The Applicant provided medical documentation of severe depression and PTSD (Annexure B1). The Respondent failed to:

  • Grant statutory sick leave for hospitalisation (Akeso Stepping Stones, March 2025)
  • Reasonably accommodate the disability (required under the Employment Equity Act, but relevant to BCEA obligations)
  • Refrain from discriminating on the basis of disability

The Respondent’s subsequent dismissal of the clinical condition as a “cynical excuse” in legal affidavits constitutes further harassment.

4.3 Certificate of Service – Section 42

The Respondent refused to provide a compliant certificate of service upon termination, in breach of Section 42.

Evidence: Correspondence requesting certificate (Annexure C‑3), CCMA outcome certificate confirming default.

6. PRAYER FOR RELIEF

The Applicant respectfully requests the following orders:

  1. A declaration that the Applicant was constructively dismissed and that the dismissal was automatically unfair in terms of Section 187(1)(h) of the LRA.
  2. Compensation equivalent to 24 months’ remuneration for the automatically unfair dismissal and occupational detriment.
  3. Payment of all outstanding salary from July 2025 to December 2025 (40.47% deduction reversed).
  4. A compliance order directing the Respondent to issue a proper certificate of service.
  5. Referral of criminal conduct (perjury, fraud, and PDA contraventions) to the National Prosecuting Authority and relevant professional bodies.
  6. Costs on an attorney‑and‑client scale due to the Respondent’s bad faith litigation conduct.
